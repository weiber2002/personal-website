Designing chips and staying active share more than you'd think — both demand meticulous preparation, the ability to push through bottlenecks, and knowing when to pace yourself. Here's what I do when I'm not staring at waveforms.

01

NTU EE Volleyball

I've been playing volleyball with the NTU EE department team (NTUEE) throughout my undergraduate years — training twice a week, competing in inter-department tournaments, and somehow finding the energy to spike after a long RTL debugging session.

Volleyball is the closest thing to parallel processing I know. Six people executing different roles simultaneously, reading the same game state, reacting in real time. A good set is like a well-timed pipeline: everyone hits their stage at exactly the right moment. Miss one transition, and the whole play collapses. Sound familiar?

We took home a tournament trophy at the inter-department competition. Not bad for a team of engineers who spend most of their time debugging Verilog.

Triathlon Finisher

51.5 km Olympic Distance
Swim · Bike · Run 3 disciplines, 1 race
Finisher ✓ No DNF. Ever.

There's a particular kind of stubbornness required to sign up for a triathlon. You swim until your arms give out, clip into a bike for 40 km in the heat, then lace up your shoes and run a 10k on legs that have already forgotten what rest feels like. I did it anyway.

Training for a triathlon while finishing a 28 nm tape-out taught me something: pacing is a system-level problem. Go too hard on the swim and you blow up your bike legs. Optimize each segment in isolation and you sub-optimize the whole. It's the same trade-off as PPA — you tune the full pipeline, not just one stage.

That photo in the transition zone? Bib #1088, right before the bike leg. The mountain shot is from a training ride up to the reservoir — one of those climbs where you earn the view on the way down. And yes, I'm still wearing my glasses on the run. Some things never change.
